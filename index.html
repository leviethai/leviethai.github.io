One dimensional version

Problem Consider a long thin water pipe. Say $\rho(x, t)$ represents the concentration of a solute at point $x$ and time $t$. Suppose the ambient solution is moving with velocity $v$ (which could depend on $x$ and $t$). Given $v$, how does $\rho$ evolve with time (ignoring diffusion, which we will address later).

Let $A$ be the cross section area, and fix $x_1 < x_2$. Let $c$ be the the amount of solute between $x_1$ and $x_2$. Note $c = A \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \rho(x,t) \, dx$. Since the solute only enters and leaves through the end points, we must have $$ A \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \delt \rho \, dx = \frac{dc}{dt} = - A [v(x_2, t) \rho(x_2, t) - v(x_1,t) \rho(x_1, t) ] $$ which leads to the PDE $$ \delt \rho + \delx (v \rho) = 0 $$

Higher dimensional version

The only extra tool needed is the Divergence Theorem.

Theorem (Divergence Theorem). If $D\subset \R^3$ is a bounded domain with nice (piecewise $C^1$) boundary, and $\rho:D \to \R^3$ is a nice (i.e. $C^1$) vector field on $D$, then $$ \int_D \dv \rho \, dV = \int_{\del D} \rho \cdot \boldsymbol{n} \, dS. $$ where $\del D$ denotes the boundary of $D$ and $\n$ the outward pointing unit normal vector on $\del D$. (Note: The left hand side is a volume integral, often written as $\iiint_D$, and the right hand side is a surface integral, often written as $\iint_{\del D}$.)

Now let $\rho$ (a scalar) be the concentration of a solute in a body of water with ambient velocity $v$ (a vector). Let $R \subset D$ be any region, and $c$ be the amount of the solute in the region $R$. Then $$ \int_R \delt \rho \, dV = \frac{d}{dt} \int_R \rho \, dV = \frac{dc}{dt} = -\int_{\del R} \rho v \cdot \n \, dS = -\int_R \dv (\rho v) \, dV. $$ (Note the $-$ sign on the right!)

Since this is true for all regions $R$, we must have \begin{equation}\label{eqnTransport} \delt \rho + \dv (\rho v) = 0 \end{equation} (Remember $v$ is a vector and $\rho$ is a scalar.) This is the transport equation.

Special Case: Constant Velocity

Consider now the special case where the velocity $v$ is constant in both space and time. In this case we use $c$ to denote the velocity field, and the transport equation \eqref{eqnTransport} reduces to $$ \delt \rho + c \cdot \grad \rho = 0. $$ By the method of characteristics the general solution of this is of the form $$ \rho(x, t) = f( x - c t). $$ Drawing a picture we quickly see that the initial density distribution preserves shape and is "transported" with velocity $c$.
